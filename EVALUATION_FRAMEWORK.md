# EIED Evaluation Framework

## Required Reasoning Chain

**Claim → Evidence → Confidence → Decision**

### Claim
What status, conclusion, eligibility determination, causal attribution, rule application, or action is being asserted?

### Evidence
What current, patient-specific, internally consistent, relevant, and authoritative information supports or contradicts the claim?

### Confidence
How strongly does the complete evidence set support the conclusion? Which specific claim objects remain unresolved?

### Decision
What action does the evidence justify: release, hold, correct, escalate, defer, exclude, include, finalize, revise, remove, apply, withhold, reopen, or close?

## Core Evaluation Pillars

1. **Evidence Integrity** - Does the evidence actually support the claim?
2. **Eligibility Determination** - Is the case eligible for the requested status or action, and have population entry, version, inclusion, exclusion, and safety gates been proven?
3. **Documentation Consistency** - Do the records agree, and are conflicts reconciled?
4. **Temporal and Longitudinal Integrity** - Did later evidence, recurrence, copied-forward language, or the full chronology invalidate or resolve a conclusion?
5. **Source Reliability** - Which source is current, patient-specific, relevant, and authoritative for the claim object?
6. **Missing Evidence** - Is required proof absent, merely attempted, pending, repeated, or unverified?
7. **Operational Readiness** - Can the workflow proceed once evidence requirements are met?
8. **Escalation Judgment** - Is release, hold, correction, escalation, or deferral proportionate?

## Recurrent Evidence Distinctions

- Evidence vs. assumption
- Current vs. stale
- Signed vs. correct
- Attached vs. relevant
- Ordered vs. completed
- Scheduled vs. confirmed
- Reported vs. documented
- Complete vs. eligible
- Present vs. authoritative
- Attempted vs. achieved
- Preliminary vs. final
- Repeated vs. independently confirmed
- Earlier valid vs. still valid
- Rule quoted vs. rule applicable
- Disease-eligible vs. safe to implement
- Patient-specific vs. wrong-patient or copied-forward

## Balanced Scoring Principle

EIED rewards accurate release as much as accurate escalation. The model should not receive credit for caution that is unsupported by the full evidence set.

High-quality performance requires:

- full-source and whole-record reconciliation;
- explicit evidence citations or references;
- confidence proportional to the evidence and specific claim object;
- recognition of unresolved uncertainty;
- correct owner assignment and next actions;
- no blanket approval and no blanket escalation;
- no guideline invocation before applicability is proven.
